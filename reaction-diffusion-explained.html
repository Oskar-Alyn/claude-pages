How it works · Reaction–Diffusion

Patterns out of chemistry

Two chemicals, one self-copying reaction, and a single trick that breaks it into spots and mazes.

Before the rules

A dish with two chemicals

Picture the screen as a shallow dish, filled edge to edge with chemical A. Drip a little chemical B into it and the two start to react. Nothing here is a creature or a particle, just chemistry spread across a grid.

The color is simply how much B is in each spot: dark where the dish is empty, bright where the reaction runs dense. Watch a few drops take hold.

Force 1 of 3

The reaction copies itself

Where B meets A, it converts that A into more B. So every bit of B helps make the next bit: a single speck doesn't just sit there, it spreads outward into fresh medium. Left to itself, B would copy its way across the entire dish.

Can't catch, the speck fades out
Self-feeding, it spreads on its own

Force 2 of 3

So why doesn't it fill the screen?

Two things hold it back. A is constantly fed back into the dish, so the medium never fully runs out, and B is constantly killed off, so it can't just pile up. Tip that balance and the reaction either floods everything or starves. Leave it on the knife's edge and it settles into a living, lasting front.

Too little killing, B floods in
Balanced, it holds its shape

Force 3 of 3

Why a pattern, and not a blob?

Here's the trick the whole thing turns on. Both chemicals spread, but at different speeds: A drifts fast and B oozes slowly. That mismatch is everything. Where they spread at the same speed, the edges blur into a flat wash. Where A outruns B, the front can't stay smooth, so it keeps breaking into spots and stripes.

Same speed, it dissolves to a wash
A outruns B, structure appears

Put it together

Nobody drew these

None of the spots and corridors are painted in by hand. They fall out of three plain forces acting at every point at once: a reaction that copies itself, a feed-and-kill that holds it in check, and two speeds of spreading that fracture it into shape.

Riding an invisible current

No particle decides where to go. Each one just follows the arrow it's standing on.

Each streak is one particle

Every line of color up there is a single particle, drifting on its own. It can't see the flock, the screen, or where it's headed. It only reads the one arrow beneath it and takes a step that way. Its color comes from which way it's flowing.

Those silky streams look planned. Nothing here plans anything, so where do they come from?

The one idea: the screen is a hidden map of arrows

Behind every particle is an invisible map of arrows: at every point on the screen, an arrow saying which way to go. The arrows are woven from smooth random noise, so neighbors mostly agree and the flow bends in long, continuous curves.

A particle never plans. It looks up the arrow under its feet, nudges toward it, and moves. Here's that hidden map made visible:

Faint arrows: the map. Bright streaks: particles following it.

What you're seeing: the streaks are just trails

Each particle is only a moving dot. The bright streak is the trail it leaves, fading a little each frame. Wipe the frame clean every step and you see bare dots; let the paint linger and short steps stretch into long ribbons. The trail is just how we draw the motion, and it carries most of the flow's look.

Shaping the map: how tightly it curls

Now you can shape the map itself. Swirliness sets how tightly the arrows curl, from one slow current that sweeps the whole screen to a froth of countless tiny whirlpools. Of every knob, this one reshapes the world the most. Drag to see it:

A map that breathes

The arrows don't have to hold still. Freeze the map and every particle traces the same channels forever. Let it churn slowly and those channels drift, so the streams keep wandering into new shapes.

Look again: the streams were already in the map

The rivers you've watched were there before any particle moved, waiting in the arrows. No particle invents a stream; each just traces a channel the map already held. And the arrows themselves are nothing more than smooth random noise.

See for yourself. Send the particles in from anywhere, a ring, a grid, two blobs, and they all slide into the same rivers. Or drag across the flow to tug the current as it runs.

How it works · Boids

A flock with no leader

Every bird is on its own, following the same three simple rules. Watch what they add up to.

Before the rules

Each arrow is one bird

Every triangle up there is a single bird, flying on its own. It points whichever way it's heading, and glows brighter when it's moving fast. None of them can see the whole flock. Each one only notices the few birds right next to it.

So no bird is ever steering the flock. It only reacts to whoever happens to be nearby. The flock is whatever those small, local reactions add up to.

Each bird follows the same three rules, many times a second. Here they are, one at a time.

Rule 1 of 3

Don't crowd

Looking only at its neighbors, a bird steers away from any that get too close, the way you shift your shoulders to avoid bumping someone on a crowded sidewalk. Without this, birds pile into the same spot. With it, they keep some breathing room.

Little spacing, they pile together
Keeps its distance, evenly spread

Rule 2 of 3

Go with the flow

Each bird turns to match the average direction of the neighbors around it, the way you fall into step with a crowd walking the same way. This is what turns a jittery swarm into smooth streams that travel together.

Everyone for themselves, aimless milling
Matches the crowd, they move as one

Rule 3 of 3

Don't get left behind

Each bird also drifts toward the middle of its neighbors. Nobody wants to be the one who wanders off alone. This is the pull that gathers scattered birds into a group and keeps the flock from coming apart.

No pull, they drift apart
Sticks together, one tight flock

The setting behind all three

How far can a bird see?

All three rules depend on one question: who counts as a neighbor? That depends on how far each bird can see. When it can barely see anything, the flock breaks into small cliques that ignore each other. When it can see across the screen, every bird reacts to every other, and they all move together.

Put it together

Nobody designed this

None of the patterns you've watched are written down anywhere. They appear because every bird is reacting to its neighbors, and thousands of those reactions at once add up to something no single bird intended.

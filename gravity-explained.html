One pull, a whole galaxy

A drifting cloud, a single rule, and it gathers itself into glowing whirlpools. Here is how.

Before the rule

Each glow is one speck

Every point of light up there is a single speck of matter, drifting on its own. It has no plan and no idea where the crowd is. All it ever does is feel a tug from every other speck.

No speck steers the whole cloud. The clumps, the orbits, the spiral arms, they are just what those countless little tugs add up to, run over and over, many times a second.

And there is only one rule. Here it is.

The only rule

Everything pulls on everything

Every speck attracts every other, and the closer two specks get, the harder they pull. That runaway is the whole engine: a slightly denser patch tugs a little harder, which draws specks closer, which makes it tug harder still, until a clump snaps together out of nothing but an even haze.

No pull, they just drift, scattered
Pull on, they fall into clumps

The twist

So why don't they all just collide?

If everything pulls together, you would expect one giant pile-up. The escape is sideways motion. A speck moving across the pull keeps missing what it is falling toward, and so it loops around instead of landing. That near miss, repeated forever, is an orbit.

All at once

A cloud winds itself into a spiral

Now give a whole scattered cloud that same one rule. The densest patch wins, pulling everything inward. As the cloud shrinks, its faint leftover spin tightens, the way a skater speeds up by drawing in their arms, and the infall winds into a glowing spiral.

Give it a stir (click or tap) and watch the whirlpool scatter, then gather itself back.

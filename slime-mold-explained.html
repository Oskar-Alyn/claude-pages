How it works · Slime Mold

A map that draws itself

No critter can see the map. Each just follows the strongest scent nearby and leaves a little scent of its own.

Before the rule

Each speck is one critter

Every glowing point up there is a single critter, crawling on its own. It can't see the network it's part of. It only smells the faint trail in the patch of ground right in front of it.

So no critter is steering the map. Each just reacts to the scent nearby. The branching web is whatever all those tiny sniff-and-step choices leave behind.

Every critter follows the same rule, many times a second. Here it is.

The one rule

Follow the strongest scent

A critter sniffs in three directions, a little to its left, straight ahead, and a little to its right, and turns toward whichever smells strongest. Then it steps forward and dabs down fresh scent of its own. Without the sniffing, critters just wander and smear an even haze. With it, they fall in behind each other and trails sharpen out of the fog.

Why it works

The trail is a shared map

Here's the twist: a critter can't tell its own trail from anyone else's. It just follows whatever scent is strongest, so every critter is reading a map that all of them are drawing at once. A few of them leave faint, lonely scribbles that fade as fast as they're made. Pour in a crowd and those trails pile onto each other, reinforce, and grow into branching veins. The map lives in the ground, not in any one critter.

A handful, faint scribbles
A crowd, veins emerge

The other half

Trails have to fade

The ground is also slowly forgetting. Every trail fades a little each moment, and that's not a flaw, it's the point. If scent never faded, every path would smell strong forever and the whole screen would flood into one bright smear. Because it fades, only the busy, well-travelled routes stay lit. Forgetting is what keeps the map sharp.

The knob that reshapes it

How far ahead do they look?

All of it turns on one question: how far ahead does each critter sniff? Look only at the ground underfoot and the map knots into a dense, restless fuzz of short connections. Sniff far down the road and the same critters stretch into long, bold, sweeping branches.

Put it together

Nobody drew this map

None of the veins you've watched are drawn anywhere. They appear because every critter is following scent and leaving scent, and millions of those exchanges add up to a map no single critter could plan.
